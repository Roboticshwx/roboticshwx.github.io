History

What caused the fall of one of the most influential empires in history?

What caused the fall of one of the most influential empires in history?

While Istanbul slept in the early hours of April 27th, 1909, Sultan Abdulhamid II boarded a westbound train. Under cover of darkness, he left the imperial capital, bringing an end to his reign.

  • “Boarded” = To step onto or enter a vehicle.
  • “Westbound” = Moving or facing toward the west.
  • “Imperial” = Relating to an empire, emperor, or supreme authority.
  • “Reign” = The period of rule by a monarch.

But the end of this powerful dynasty had been brewing for years. In the late 18th century, the Ottomans’ once-feared faction of elite warriors called the Janissaries had become inefficient and greedy. Their numbers swelled with poorly trained fighters, and their outdated weapons led to a series of embarrassing defeats that coincided with several economic crises.

  • “Brewing” = A liquid slowly developing or steeping.
    • Problems were building gradually over decades, not sudden cllapse, but a simmering crisis.
  • “Ottomans’ once-feared faction”
    • “Ottomans” = 奥斯曼帝国
    • “Once-feared” = Previously inspired terror.
    • “Faction” = An organized subgroup within the empire(here, the Janissaries).
  • “Elite” = Select, superior, or highly trained group.
  • “Janissaries” = 土耳其新军，也叫做杰尼西斯士兵
  • “Inefficient” = Wasteful, underperforming, failing to act effectively.
  • “Swelled” = Grew larger or puffed up.
  • “Coincided” = Events happened simultaneously, often implying a causal link.

To survive, Sultan Selim III sought to curb Janissaries’ influence and modernize the empire with widespread reforms. The warriors rebelled and assassinated Selim, but his successor would not be so easily beaten.

  • “Sultan” = 苏丹（某些伊斯兰国家统治者的称号）
  • “Curb” = To restrain, control or limit.
  • “Rebelled” = Organized violent resistance against authority.
  • “Successor” = One who follows another in power.

Mahmud II subdued the Janissaries in a massacre known as the Auspicious Event, and swiftly replaced the outmoded warriors with a European-style army.

Sultan Abdulmejid ushered in an era of reforms known as the Tanzimat, or “reordering”, where economic and administrative systems were overhauled, and usbects from all backgrounds were recruited to work for the government. The Tanzimat was hindered by foreign debt and ploitical upheavals.

Biology and Nature

How smart are crows?

How smart are crows? - Katharina Brecht

“The mimicker, the tool maker, the strutter around the bird feeder, the pretty thief of shiny objects.”

  • “Mimicker” = A creature that imitates or copies sounds. From verb to mimic.
  • “Strutter” = A creature that walks in a proud, stiff way, often to show off. From the verb to strut.

In on of Aesop’s 2500-year-old fables, a crow is searching for water. It spies a pitcher and discovers liquid inside but it’s beyond reach. Soon, the crow begins dropping pebbles into the pitcher. One by one, the displace the water, and the crow quenches its thirst.

  • “Fables” = A short story with animal characters teaching a moral. (寓言)
  • “Pitcher” = A container with handle, used for liquids.
  • “Quench” = To satisfy thirst by drinking.
    • “Reading travel blogs quenched his desire for adventure while saving money.”

Interestingly, this fable turns out to be pretty accurate. In a 2014 experiment, scientists placed a food reward in a tube partially filled with water, and watched as crow after crow dropped stone after stone in until the food was in beak-snatching distance. This is just one of many fascinating displays of intelligence from corvids, the bold, brainy family of songbirds, that includes crows, ravens, jays, and magpies. To start, corvids are strategic when it comes to their food. They ransack trash bins and snatch bites from under other birds’ beaks. They also hoard and cache their food.

  • “beak-snatching”
    • “Beak” = The hard, pointed mouth part of a bird.(鸟喙)
    • “Snatch” = Grabbing something suddenly, quickly and often aggressively.
    • “Corvids” refer to the entire bird family “Corvidae”(鸦科).
    • “Songbirds” = A group of birds known for their vocal abilities, including many species that are capable of producing complex sounds and melodies.(鸣禽)
      • crows 乌鸦
      • ravens 渡鸦
      • jays 松鸦
      • magpies 喜鹊
  • “Bold” = Fearless and daring; willing to take risks or act decisively.
    • The bold raccoon ripped open the camper’s cooler.
  • “Ransack” implies savage theft and disarray beyond mere searching.
  • “Hoard” = To gather and hiddenly stockpile valuable resources, often obsessively or greedily.

A single Clark’s nutcracker, for instance, is thought to scatter some 30,000 pine nuts across more than 2,000 cache sites, which it can recover more than nine months later. And in one experiment, scrub jays given waxworms and peanuts, cached and retrieved the perishable worms first and left the peanuts for later.

  • “Clark’s nutcracker”: 北美星鸦
  • “Scrub jays”: 灌丛鸦’
  • “Waxworms”: 蜡虫.
  • “Scatter” = To disperse widely across a landscape.
  • “Pine nuts” refer specifically to the edible seeds produced by pine trees(松树).
  • “Perishable” refers to items that spoil, decay or become inedible quickly if not consumed or preserved promptly. (易腐烂的)

Corvid abilities extend beyond meal prepping.

  • “Prepping” refers to the planning, preparing, and managing resources in advance.

Corvids like New Caledonian crows, also demonstrate an impressive proclivity for reasoning and tool use. Even in the wild, they fashion sticks into hook-shaped probes for food.

  • “New Caledonian crows”: 新克里多尼亚乌鸦
  • “Proclivity” refers to a natural or habitual inclination toward something—-especially a behavior, skill, or tendency that feels instinctive or innate.(倾向)
  • “Fashion” (as a verb) = To skillfully create, shape, or modify raw materials into a functional or deliberate form.
  • “Hook-shaped” = Having a curved, J-like end(钩状) for snagging or lifting.
  • “Probes” = Tools for inspecting, extracting, or manipulating objects.(探针/探具)

But corvids also do things that are harder to explain. They sometimes tote trinkets around, which might help them practice caching or serve as gifts to their partners. And they’ve been spotted repeatedly sledding down roofs on plastic lids. Play activities like this are relatively common in corvids, especially juvenils.

  • “Tote” = To carry something casually, often over a distance.
  • “Trinkets” = Small, decorative items of little practical value, often bright, shiny, or textured.
  • “Sledding” = Sliding down a slope using an object as a makeshift vehicle(滑橇), not accidental slipping, but intentional play.
  • “Lids” = Lightweight plastic or covers from human-made containers.
  • “Juveniles” = Young individuals of a species, especially not yet sexually mature.

They breed cooperatively; live and communicate in large dynamic groups; share food; and mob predators together.

  • “Breed” = To produce offspring, but specifically here in a socially complex, collaborative system.(群体养殖)
  • “Mob” = To group aggressively against a threat.

Scientists today consider corvids among the smartest animals – a big step from where consensus used to be. In the late 1800s, German scientists Ludwig Edinger, compared primate and crow brains and interpreted the crow’s brain structure as primitive, dismissing their cognitive abilities. This was a misinterpretation. A raven, for example, has a brain that looks quite different that a Capuchin monkeys, and is about a third of the size. But both animals have a similar number of neurons in their pallium, a brain region that’s associated with cognition.

  • “Consensus” = General agreement among experts in a field, formed after debate and evidence evaluation.
  • “Primate” = Mammalian order including humans, apes, monkeys.(灵长类)
  • “Interpreted” = To subjectively explain data based on existing beliefs.
  • “Primitive” = Early, undeveloped, simple. Implies evolutionary backwardness.
  • “Dismissing” = To reject as unworthy of consideration.
  • “Misinterpretation” = A flawed conclusion from data due to bias or limited methods.
  • “Capuchin monkey”: 卷尾猴
  • “Pallium”：大脑皮层

Sociology

How did detectives solve the case of the bloody fingerprints?

How did detectives solve the case of the bloody fingerprints?

On October 17th, 1902, Paris detectives arrived at a grisly scene.

  • “Grisly” = Causing intense horror, disgust, or revulsion, typically due to explicit brutality or bloodshed. (血腥，恐怖)

With no eyewitnesses, the officers had little to work from, until they discovered a shard of broken glass with several bloody fingerprints.

  • “Shard” specifically means a sharp, fragmented piece of brittle material.

Henri Scheffer, who had been arrested for theft the year before, was detained and later confessed to the murder.

  • “Detained” = Legally held in custody for investigation, without formal charges yet.
  • “Confessed” = Formally admitted guilt to the crime.

Humans, like other climbing species, are born with patterns of raised ridges and recessed furrows, not only on their fingers but also along their hands and feet. These friction ridges help provide a firmer grip, especially on wet surfaces, and increase our sensitivity to touch. They begin to develop in the womb at about seven weeks, when the skin on the hands and feet swell to form smooth raised pads. At around ten weeks, deeper layers begin to grow faster than the upper layers, casuing the pads to buckle and fold. This folding is guided by genetics and environmental factors, and continues over the next several months.

  • “Ridges” = Raised lines of skin that form distinct patterns. (凸纹)
  • “Furrows” = The valleys or grooves between ridges. (沟纹)
  • “Recessed” = Set below the surrounding surface; indented or sunken. (凹陷的)
    • “Liquid pools in the recessed furrows” = Liquid collects in the skin valleys.
  • “Grip” = The ability to grasp or hold onto something firmly.
  • “Womb” = The organ in a female mammal where offspring develop before birth. (子宫)
  • “Swell” = To expand or enlarge due to internal pressure or growth.
  • “Pads” = Soft, thickened areas of skin that act as cushions. (肉垫)
  • “Buckle” = To bend, fold, or collapse under pressure.

In fact, many experts believe it’s improbable that any two fingerprints, past or present, are exactly alike. They’re even different among identical twins.

  • “Improbable” = Highly unlikely to happen; almost certain not to occur.
  • “Identical” = Exactly the same in every detail; indistinguishable.

By the early 1900s, prosecutors began utilizing fingerprints in courts, forever changing how detectives approach and analyze crime scenes.

  • “Prosecutors” = Legal representatives who bring criminal charges against a defendant in court.

These include patent prints, left by transferable substances like blood or dirt, and plastic prints, which are impressions in soft, malleable materials, like caulking, wax, or even a newly painted fence.

  • “Patent prints” = Visible, easily noticeable fingerprints.
  • “Transferable substances” = Materials that stick to skin and deposit onto surfaces.
  • “Plastic prints” = 3D impressions left in soft materials.
  • “Malleable materials” = Surfaces that deform under pressure and soft enough to retain ridge detail when touched.
  • “Caulking” = Soft sealant used in construction. (用于施工的软密封材料)
  • “Wax” = Soft, moldable substance.(蜡)
  • “Fence” = A barrier enclosing an area, often made of wood or metal. (栅栏)

These are called latent prints, and they’re composed of the water, oils, proteins, and salts, that ridge patterns leave on surfaces. They can be revealed by dusting with a fine powder, which sticks to the water and oil trail.

  • “Latent” = Existing but no visible, hidden, requiring specific conditions to become detectable.
  • “Trail” = A path, track, a series of marks left behind.

If surfaces are porous or difficult to dust, investigators use chemical developers like Ninhydrin, which reacts with proteins left by fingers.

  • “Porous” = A material containing tiny holes or capillaries that absorb liquids or gases. (e.g. paper, cardboard, raw wood, fabric)
  • “Ninhydrin” = 茚三酮

Forensic scientists continue to develop more sensitive and surface-specific tools. For example, one experimental technique uses an electrical charge to capture the corrosion that fingerprint salts leave on metals, even if they have been wiped down.

  • “Forensic” = Relating to the scientific methods to investigate crimes, analyze evidence, and provide data for legal proceedings.
  • “Corrosion” = Electrochemical degradation of metals due to reactions with their environment.

Certified experts then compare minor fingerprint details, such as how the ridges branch and intersect, and the spacing of oil pores.

  • “Pores” = Microscopic openings (15-50μm wide) on fingerprint ridges where sweat ducts emerge to secrete. (毛孔，气孔)
    • “The spacing of oil pores” = 毛孔之间的距离

Prints are often smudged, distorted, or overlapped with other prints, which can make matching more challenging. And it’s prone to human error.

  • “Smudged” = Ridge patterns dragged or wiped across a surface due to lateral finger movement or excessive residue, causing blurred or unreadable areas. (弄脏，涂污)
  • “Distorted” = Twisted, warped, or stretched out of its true shape.Unevene force sculpts ridges into unnatural shapes or stretch and compress.
  • “Prone” = Likely to experience or naturally inclined to suffer from something negative.
    • The fingerprint-matching process is naturally vulnerable to mistakes by experts.

While this may seem low, the stakes are high for a wrongly accused defendant. Futher, there’s no universal standard on the number of ridge details that experts must analyze to make a match. Many investigators stress that no conviction should be made on fingerprint evidence alone.

  • “Stakes” = What can be gained or lost in a situation; the level of risk or importance involved.
  • “Defendant” = The person accused of a crime in a court.
  • “Conviction” = A formal declaration that someone is guilty of a crime following a trial.

Psychology

How to get motivated even when you don’t feel like it

You’ve always aspired to be a professional artist. At last, this dream may become a reality, and you’re creating a portfolio to submit to art programs. But as the application deadline looms, you suddenly find yourself unmotivated, and avoiding the canvas altogether.

Why does motivation seem so fickle? And what even is it in the first place?

Psychologists define motivation as the desire or impetus to initiate and maintain a particular behavior. In other words, it’s the energy that drives you to do something. And knowing the source of that drive is particularly important when it comes to understanding how to maintain it.

These motivational forces generally fall into two broad categories: intrinsic and extrinsic. Intrinsic motivation is involved when you experience an activity as an end in itself. Take a hobby, like playing video games. The experience largely explains the desire to do it. Performing tasks that feel right in the moment or that you find a meaningful, interesting, or satisfying are driven by intrinsic motivation.

Extrinsic motivation, on the other hand, refers to pursuing a task as a means to an end. While few would consider going to the dentist as an enjoyable activity, you’re often motivated by the outcome of having clean, healthy teeth. Other examples of extrinsic motivation include completing a task to receive some sort of reward, whether it’s praise, power, or money. Notably, these rewards tend to come later, like receiving a bonus at the end of a quarter, or winning a competition after months of training. While extrinsic rewards, like getting paid, may seem appealing, their effectiveness can be surprisingly short-lived.

For example, a 2017 study found that those who were highly focused on the outcomes of their New Year’s resolutions or driven by extrinsic motivation weren’t the most likely to stick to them. What did predict persistence, however, was how much a person enjoyed pursuing their goals. In other words, you’re more likely to maintain an exercise routine if you take classes you enjoy, rather than just those that build your biceps.

Years of psychology research have shown that high levels of intrinsic motivation for school, a job, or an exercise class are more likely to keep you engaged in the long run. Day-to-day actions, though, are rarely either exclusively intrinsically motivated. Study for histroy exam, for example, can be intrinsically motivated if you’re curious about the culture of ancient Egypt. But extrinsic motivators may also be at play, as you aim to get good grade or feel pressure from family members to do well in school. But having multiple motivators isn’t always better.

One study of military cadets found that those who were driven both by intrinsic motivators, like self-improvement, and extrinsic motivators, like the outcome of getting a good job, were overall less motivated than cadets driven by just one of these factors. As a result, these cadets performed worse and were less likely to graduate. Psychologists call this phenomenon the overjustification effect, the idea that additional extrinsic motivators can actually muddy the waters when you already have the intrinsic drive to do something.

But this is only a problem if you already find a task motivating. When you’re faced with an activity you find tedious or uninteresing, adding extrinsic rewards can be beneficial. In this way, extrincis motivators can provide sufficient justification. While you may never enjoy doing the laundry, it may feel less daunting if you get praise from a loved one, or even promise yourself that you’ll watch your favorite TV show when you’re done folding.

Motication is complicated. And sometimes, no matter how passionate you are about a goal or hobby, finding the motivation to actually do it can be difficult. But there are things you can do to increase your drive, even when it feels impossible. Focus on building intrinsic motivation by making the task more fun in the moment. Asking a friend to join you or simply putting on your favorite playlist can give you the boost to get started and stick with your goals for the long haul.
